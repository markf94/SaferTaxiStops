When you hover your mouse over the road segments in the map you will find the data we collected on the routes. This data was used to create maps that distinguish route segments on which taxi drivers are prone to get fined for stopping, and areas that offer 'safe' stopping places.


        NOT SAFE TO STOP *

A red route segment means there is
- a traffic light, bus stop, bridge, red line, roundabout or  pedestrian crossing
- the left lane is not wide enough for a vehicle to stop without obstructing traffic (i.e., less than 1.5 times the size of a standard lane)

        SAFE TO STOP

Road segments where motorised vehicles are allowed to stop are displayed in green .


*These are the cases defined as no-stopping zones for motorised vehicles by the South African traffic rules. Taxi drivers frequently get fined for stopping in any of those areas. Please note that according to eThekwini's Public Transport By-Law of 2014 minibus taxis are in fact only allowed to stop "at a rank, special parking place or a stop designated for that type of vehicle" to pick up or drop off passengers.

Sandile drives from Gateway to Market

“We as taxi drivers face real problems every day and that is because the government doesn’t like the taxi industry.”


Sandile Zulu* drives a 22 seater minibus taxi for his boss who belongs to the Umhlanga Taxi Association. This week, Sandile is driving the route from Gateway shopping center to Durban in the Market area. This is a fill-up-and-go route, meaning that the taxi fills up with commuters at Gateway and drops them off in Durban on the way to or at the Market. This route doesn’t collect passengers along the way.

Zulu also drives the local route, collecting people in and around Umhlanga and bringing them to the Gateway rank. He says that there are not enough taxi stops, especially in Umhlanga. “We as taxi drivers face real problems every day and that is because the government doesn’t like the taxi industry”. Even though the Umhlanga area has recently been developed, the government has not provided enough places for taxis to stop safely and legally when collecting passengers. Many roads are only 1 lane roads allowing no space for other vehicles to pass around them, when they stop for passengers. He said, “The city needs to create more safe spots for taxis to stop around the city and area outside of the city.”

The association then fines the driver R300 if a passenger complains about them.

Passengers are encouraged to report bad driving to the association. And they do so. Zulu said, “Passengers shout at us if we don’t stop where they want us to stop, even if it is unsafe. And sometimes report us at the office”. The association then fines the driver R300 if a passenger complains about them.

The police also give the taxi drivers fines for stopping at unsafe and illegal places. Zulu said that he has an outstanding fine of R3000.

On an average day, Zulu drives about 5- 6 trips per day. Most of his time is spent waiting for the taxi to fill up at the rank. He works 6 days a week and never on Sundays. He said, “Sometimes ngisebenza 5 days ama ubaba sebenza efemini engibambele” meaning he sometimes works only 5 days if his friend who works at a firm is available to assist him. The minimum he earns a week is R1200 but most often it is more.

Zulu said, “Being a taxi driver is not a stressful job but can sometimes come with attitude”. In his experience, motorists don’t have respect for taxi drivers, and he feels that they look down upon taxi drivers.

*Name changed to protect source’s identity.

  • Durban motorists would like to see taxis have more safe places to stop as this will make the roads safer for all users. A quick vox pop survey revealed the following views of city motorists.

    Generally if I am polite with them they tend to be polite back to me but a lot of the time they do just cut in front of you without even looking or they push in front of you which makes driving dangerous. I do think that they should definitely have more places to park. They do just stop anywhere.

    - Berea Motorist

    Coming from Joburg I have gotten used to the whole taxi driving thing but here in Durban they like are ridiculous. On my way to work in the morning I come across a whole bunch of them. Some of them give you a little bit of space to go past them and others stop right in the middle of the road for no apparent reason so it is kind of annoying. One thing I think they might consider doing is like I saw on the one of highways, there is like a dedicated bus lane. If they could do that for the taxis for parking that would be amazing.

    - Westville Motorist

    I no longer get upset with taxi drivers. I just feel that they need to be a little bit more courteous. They do give way to us at times and they do show respect but I would say the majority don't. As far as the passengers demanding that they can just drop them anywhere, that shouldn’t be but we definitely need to be a lot more accommodating towards the taxi drivers. We are living in a city where the main mode of transport is taxis. We know that the economy is suffering and this is a cheaper form of transport. It is very convenient and they get dropped off right near their work and more and more of this is going to be happening as people move into the taxi mode of transport. The police are going to have to be a bit more lenient on this.

    - Glenmore Motorist
