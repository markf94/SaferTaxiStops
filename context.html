Why do minibus taxis have nowhere to stop?


This timeline shows how the minibus taxi industry was born out of the desperate need to bring workers to work despite being forced to live on the outskirts of town during apartheid. From the get go, these transport providers have had to negotiate, claim and fight for their right to operate in South Africa. Since 1994, the taxi industry has grown from only 8000 registered taxis to 298 000 today, employing approximately 600 000 people and worth R40billion.

On the one hand, it is celebrated as an industry of self-made, predominantly black entrepreneurs, and on the other hand, it is a force to be reckoned with. The market is now over-saturated and there is little room for expansion or growth. There seems to be a stale mate between different actors in taxi industry and different local government actors. People on both sides want the Integrated Public Transport Plan (IRPTN) to invest in inter-modal public transport system that incorporates the taxi industry as a feeder network. But there are also skeptics who don't believe that the taxi operators or drivers will be fully compensated when the busses are implemented.

In the meantime, there aren't enough legal taxi stops for minibus taxis to fetch and drop off their passengers and the city will not invest in building new taxi stops when they have a mandate from council to implement the IRPTN. At the same time, the Metro Police who fine the taxi drivers for stopping in bus stops, do not work with the Department of Transport to better manage the existing use of public transport facilities and roads in the city.
